How I built Fastlytics

Subhashh Gottumukkala
05 Apr 2025 · 8 min read

I’ve been an F1 fan since I was nine years old. The speed, the strategy, and the tiny decisions that make or break a race fascinated me. Fastlytics grew out of that obsession—the idea was simple: what if every fan had access to telemetry-grade data without needing a pit-wall budget?

Over the past six months I pulled together streaming lap telemetry, weather feeds, and historical race events into a single pipeline. The challenge wasn’t just crunching data, it was creating an experience that helped fans build intuition. Every chart needed to highlight the “why”, not just the “what”.

Designing a race-native experience

Early prototypes felt like spreadsheets. Useful, but uninspiring. I switched to a dark, track-inspired palette, leaned on blur effects, and collaborated with a motion designer to inject micro-animations when a driver set a purple sector. The result is a dashboard that feels alive, but never distracts from the racing narrative.

Scaling the analytics stack

Fastlytics runs on a serverless ingestion layer. Lap streams are captured via AWS Kinesis, enriched with contextual race data, and persisted into ClickHouse for sub-second aggregations. The frontend consumes a GraphQL API that fuses fan-friendly copy with raw stats, keeping pages responsive even during live events.

The biggest engineering lift was building a predictive model to flag undercut opportunities. By combining historical tire degradation curves with live sector deltas we can surface strategy calls before teams pull the trigger. It’s the closest thing fans have to sitting on the timing stand.

Lessons for fellow builders

Ship small, narrate the story, and invite feedback early. Fastlytics went from a weekend experiment to a community of thousands because I opened the doors early and shared the build process. If you’re crafting a data-heavy product, focus first on the narrative your users care about—technology should amplify it, not drown it out.
